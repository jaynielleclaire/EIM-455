1. Introduction

Orthopedic surgeries often require intermittent use of pulsed fluoroscopy (Fig 1)—real-time X-ray imaging—at various points throughout the procedure. Such procedures include hip fracture, knee replacement, minimally invasive surgeries, and hand surgeries [1]. This imaging involves taking numerous radiation "shots" or pulses, typically around a hundred in a single surgery, though the exact number depends on the surgeon’s expertise; more experienced surgeons may require fewer images [1]. Prolonged exposure to such radiation over years can increase the risk of thyroid cancer, making protective measures essential. For instance, using a thyroid shield can reduce effective radiation dose by approximately 2.5 times and overall exposure by nearly 50% [2].

During fluoroscopy, the surgical team members—including the surgeon, radiologic technician, surgical assistant, and scrub nurse—wear personal protective equipment (PPE) like thyroid shields (Fig 2) and lead gowns (typically 0.5 mm thick, fully wrapped as seen in Fig 3) to minimize exposure. Other team members, such as the circulating nurse and anaesthesiologist, usually stay behind a lead shield (Fig 4) when imaging is active. The layout of personnel around the operating table is often arranged to optimize both access and protection, as shown in Fig 5.

Lead gowns are composed of three layers:

  1. Interior lining (canvas): A soft material providing comfort as it is the layer closest to the user's body.
  2. Radiation protective material (lead): The core layer responsible for shielding the user from radiation.
  3. Exterior material (vinyl or nylon): The outermost, durable, and water-resistant layer that protects the radiation shielding material from damage and wear [1].

Lead gowns are highly effective, blocking 90-95% of scattered radiation [3]. However, they are quite heavy, ranging from 2 to 7 kilograms [4], and wearing them for extended periods can lead to fatigue and musculoskeletal strain [5]. This project, therefore, seeks to improve the usability and comfort of lead aprons to reduce these physical burdens while maintaining their protective function.



2. Problem Clarification

2.1 Key Insights

Our primary research with healthcare professionals (HCP) highlighted critical issues regarding weight, hygiene, and mobility in current radiation PPE. Below is a breakdown of each category based on practitioners' feedback:

  • Weight: The heavy lead aprons, often weighing up to 7kg, create significant physical strain on practitioners, leading to muscle fatigue and back pain.

  • Hygiene: Shared lead aprons bring about hygiene concerns, as practitioners are uncertain about how often these are cleaned. Due to the intense physical activity involved, wearing aprons underneath scrubs leads to excessive sweating, which raises discomfort levels. Practitioners indicated that cleaning these aprons between uses isn't always feasible, adding to the hygiene issue.

  • Mobility: The bulkiness and weight of the PPE restrict practitioners' movement, making it challenging to perform certain procedures.

Among the issues identified, the weight of lead aprons emerged as a top concern, significantly impacting comfort and safety during prolonged procedures. Notably, 100% of interviewees from the orthopaedics department cited the weight of the aprons as an issue (Fig. 6).

2.2 Problem Statement

The weight of the lead apron worn by orthopaedic surgical teams during fluoroscopy-guided surgeries causes significant fatigue, compromising their well-being and quality of life. This physical strain also limits the number of surgeries they can perform each week, impacting both healthcare delivery and practitioner health.

2.3 Existing Solutions

2.3.1 Xenolite Gowns

Xenolite gowns provide lead-free, lightweight, and flexible x-ray protection by using a carefully balanced mix of antimony and tungsten as shielding elements. This combination is designed to optimize weight while maximizing protection within the diagnostic imaging range of 80–100 kV. Antimony primarily attenuates lower-energy photons (below 88 keV), while tungsten, a heavier element, is effective against higher-energy radiation (above 69 keV). The powdered antimony and tungsten particles are uniformly embedded in a durable yet flexible Dow elastomer matrix, developed by Dow-DuPont for applications requiring resilience and flexibility, like running shoes and cables. This elastomer was chosen for its balance of strength and flexibility, enhancing the apron’s durability and resistance to cracking. Furthermore, the material is environmentally friendly as it is not cross-linked, meaning it can be recycled or safely disposed of as non-hazardous waste [11]. However, Xenolite provides a lead equivalency of 0.5 mm at 80 keV but may offer less protection at higher energy levels (e.g., 100 keV as seen in Table 2), where advanced imaging equipment is often used [5].

2.3.2 Suspended Radiation Protection System (Zero-Gravity)

Zero-Gravity is a weightless support system that uses a suspended, 1.0 mm lead shield, magnetically attached to a vest worn by the operator. This design allows the system to move smoothly with the user and be easily retracted when not in use. Available in various configurations—such as floor unit, monorail, and hinged swing-arm versions—Zero-Gravity can adapt to different room layouts and procedure requirements. By eliminating the need for heavy protective gear, it minimizes the risk of orthopaedic strain for clinicians, enhancing comfort without compromising radiation protection [14]. Although Zero-Gravity allows some movement, it restricts certain actions like bending and can limit mobility, especially when walking [1].

4. Concept: "LatticeGuard Pro"

The "LatticeGuard Pro" is an advanced protective gown that combines material optimization with cutting-edge design to balance radiation shielding, durability, and user comfort. At its core is a 3D lattice structure, crafted from high-density lightweight alloys, which offers significant weight reduction while maintaining exceptional structural strength and flexibility.

4.1 Key Features

  1. 3D Lattice Framework:

    • Weight Reduction:The lattice design employs a network of geometric patterns (e.g., hexagonal, grid, or triangular) to distribute material only where needed, reducing bulk and enhancing flexibility.
    • High Strength-to-Weight Ratio: The framework provides a strong yet lightweight base, making the gown easier to wear for extended periods.
    • Reinforced Points: Critical load-bearing areas, such as the shoulders and spine, are strengthened to prevent deformation and sagging under prolonged use.

  2. Radiation Shielding:

    • The lattice structure is paired with lead-based infill material, such as sheeting or compressed granules, strategically placed within the lattice's openings. This hybrid construction ensures effective radiation protection while keeping the gown lightweight.
    • Layered Depth: For enhanced shielding, a triangular lattice design with multi-layered depth may be used, optimizing impact resistance and stability.

  3. Comfort and Mobility:

    • Elastic Panels: Flexible fabric inserts enhance freedom of movement, allowing users to perform complex tasks without restriction.
    • Weight Distribution: A load-bearing belt and cross-body strap system reduce strain by evenly distributing weight, minimizing fatigue during extended use.
    • Ventilation and Padding: The inner lining features pressure-relieving foam for comfort and mesh ventilation panels to promote airflow and temperature regulation.

  4. User Convenience:

    • Customizable Fit: Adjustable straps and closures, along with Velcro strips, allow quick detachment and a tailored fit.
    • Breathability: The lattice's inherent gaps improve air circulation, reducing overheating and enhancing wearability.

4.2 Benefits of a 3D Lattice Structure

  1. Efficient Material Use:

    • Reduces unnecessary bulk by targeting high-stress areas for reinforcement while maintaining lightweight low-stress zones.

  2. Flexibility and Comfort:

    • Enhances wearability by conforming to body movements and evenly distributing pressure.
    • Improves user satisfaction by mitigating discomfort and strain associated with traditional lead aprons.

  3. Durability:

    • Reinforced lattice points add structural integrity, ensuring the gown lasts at least five years with regular use.

4.3 Proposed Design Variants

  • Hexagonal Lattice with Reinforced Corners: Focuses on maximizing flexibility and strength with strategically reinforced intersections near the shoulders and waist.

  • Grid Lattice with Diagonal Beams: Adds diagonal reinforcements within each square grid to improve stability and prevent sagging under load.

  • Triangular Lattice with Multi-Layer Depth: Provides enhanced impact resistance and durability with reinforced points at every corner, ideal for heavy-duty applications.

4.4 Assembly Process

  1. The lattice structure is manufactured as an integrated unit using 3D printing or molding techniques.
  2. Radiation shielding material is inserted into lattice openings, either as pre-formed sheets or as granules that are compressed to fit.
  3. The completed composite provides optimal strength, flexibility, and protection in a lightweight design.

By merging state-of-the-art material science with ergonomic design, the LatticeGuard Pro exemplifies a thoughtful approach to addressing the dual needs of protection and comfort in radiation PPE. It offers a durable, lightweight, and user-friendly solution for orthopedic surgical teams while maintaining compliance with current radiation protection standards.



5. Prototyping and Testing

The evaluation of any proposed solution must address two fundamental criteria:

  1. Radiation Protection Efficacy: The solution must provide radiation protection comparable to current lead aprons
  2. Ergonomic Performance: The solution must optimize user comfort through improved weight distribution and overall design

5.1 Material Selection Using Linear Attenuation

To evaluate radiation protection efficacy, we conducted a comprehensive analysis of linear attenuation properties and potential weight reduction compared to conventional lead aprons. Our analysis encompassed a range of potential materials including Bismuth, Tungsten, Copper, Zinc, Iron, Magnesium, Aluminium, and Titanium (detailed calculations available in Appendix G). Testing conditions were standardized at 0.5mm thickness and 100keV incident radiation energy, which represents typical diagnostic X-ray conditions.

While Tungsten demonstrated superior attenuation compared to pure lead, its significantly higher density (19.3 g/cm³ versus lead's 11.4 g/cm³) rendered it impractical for wearable protection despite its excellent shielding properties.

The most promising alternative composition identified was a 90% lead and 10% bismuth mixture, which achieved an attenuation ratio of 0.0311, compared to pure lead's 0.0315. This combination offers a modest weight reduction of 1.37% while maintaining near-equivalent radiation protection. However, the slight decrease in attenuation efficiency, though minimal, requires careful consideration given the critical nature of radiation protection in medical settings. Further optimization of the material composition may be necessary to achieve equivalent protection while maximizing weight reduction.

5.2 Usability Testing Protocol and Methodology

The usability testing protocol has been designed to comprehensively evaluate the ergonomic performance of our prototype apron against traditional lead aprons in a simulated clinical environment. Our testing framework encompasses both quantitative measurements and qualitative user feedback to ensure a thorough evaluation of the design's effectiveness.

5.2.1 Research Objectives

  • Primary Question: How does the comfort level and usability of the prototype apron compare to traditional lead aprons in clinical settings?
  • Secondary Questions:
    • How does extended wear affect user fatigue patterns?
    • What are the perceived ergonomic benefits compared to traditional designs?
    • Which specific design elements contribute most to user comfort?
    • How do physiological responses differ between the two designs?

5.2.2 Experimental Design and Participant Selection

The study will involve 10-15 healthcare professionals, specifically selecting from practicing orthopaedic surgeons, nurses, and radiologists with experience using traditional lead aprons.

Participant Requirements:

  • Have used traditional lead aprons before
  • Currently practising in orthopaedics, nursing, or radiology
  • No pre-existing musculoskeletal conditions
  • Varied body types and experience levels for comprehensive assessment

5.2.3 Testing Equipment and Materials

Our testing will utilize specialized equipment to gather precise data on user experience and physiological responses:

Essential Equipment:

  • Prototype apron with modular weight distribution system
  • Traditional lead apron (control)
  • Medical-grade heart rate monitor
  • Digital grip strength dynamometer
  • Motion capture system for posture analysis
  • EMG sensors for muscle fatigue assessment

Measurement Tools:

  • Comfort assessment questionnaires (5-point Likert scale)
  • Standardized fatigue evaluation forms
  • Semi-structured interview protocols

5.2.4 Testing Protocol Implementation

The testing protocol consists of three distinct phases:

  • Phase 1: Baseline Assessment (15 minutes)
    • Initial physiological measurements
    • Equipment familiarization
    • Collection of demographic data

  • Phase 2: Comparative Testing (30 minutes per apron)
    • Each participant will complete a sequence of controlled activities:
      • Standing procedures (5 minutes)
      • Simulated surgical movements (10 minutes)
      • Recovery period (5 minutes)
      • Repeated movement sequence (10 minutes)
    • During Phase 2, continuous monitoring will track:
      • Heart rate variations
      • Grip strength changes
      • Posture deviations
      • Real-time comfort ratings

  • Phase 3: Post-Test Analysis
    • This final phase focuses on gathering comprehensive feedback after the testing period:
      • Immediate post-wear comfort assessment
      • Completion of detailed ergonomic questionnaire
      • Semi-structured interviews for in-depth feedback
      • Comparative analysis between prototype and traditional apron
      • Documentation of any physical stress indicators
      • Collection of specific design improvement suggestions

5.2.5 Performance Metrics

Our analysis combines quantitative and qualitative measurements to provide comprehensive insights:

Quantitative Parameters:

  • Physiological responses (heart rate, muscle fatigue)
  • Postural analysis data
  • Comfort scores (1-5 scale)
  • Time-to-fatigue measurements

Qualitative Assessments:

  • User experience narratives
  • Design improvement suggestions
  • Workflow impact feedback

5.2.6 Expected Outcomes and Success Criteria

We anticipate that the prototype apron will demonstrate measurable improvements over traditional designs. Success will be determined by achieving specific performance targets:

Primary Success Metrics:

  • Minimum 20% reduction in reported discomfort scores
  • Statistically significant improvement in posture maintenance
  • Reduced physiological stress indicators
  • Maintained radiation protection effectiveness

Secondary Benefits:

  • Enhanced mobility during procedures
  • Improved task performance
  • Positive user feedback regarding weight distribution
  • Identification of further optimization opportunities

Through this comprehensive testing protocol, we aim to validate our design improvements and identify any areas requiring refinement before proceeding to final production. The combination of quantitative data and qualitative feedback will provide crucial insights for optimizing the prototype's design to meet the demanding needs of healthcare professionals in real-world clinical settings.



6. Future Work

Moving forward, we will continue to refine and validate the feasibility of the LatticeGuard Pro concept by collaborating with materials scientists and radiation physicists/medical physicists. This exploration will focus on optimizing material properties, including density and weight distribution, to achieve the desired balance between radiation shielding effectiveness and user comfort. Should challenges arise that prevent the implementation of LatticeGuard Pro, we will pivot to the SuspendShield Flex concept.

The concept decision will be finalized by December 2024, ensuring a clear direction for the next development phase.

Once the final concept is selected, usability testing will commence from January to March 2025. These tests will assess user comfort, ease of movement, and long-term wearability. Additionally, the feedback collected during this phase will inform iterative design improvements.

Key milestones in 2025 will include:

  • Prototype Refinement: Incorporating usability feedback into the prototype to enhance performance and comfort.
  • Advanced Testing: Collaborating with orthopaedic surgical teams to simulate real-world scenarios and validate the effectiveness of weight distribution mechanisms.
  • Regulatory Preparation: Consulting with regulatory experts to ensure compliance with radiation protection standards and medical device guidelines.
  • Material Scalability Assessment: Evaluating the cost and scalability of selected materials for eventual production, particularly for broader market adoption beyond the initial Singapore focus.

Finally, we aim to present the finalized prototype and testing results by April 2025, positioning the product for further commercialization and industry engagement.

    • Material Optimization:

    • High-Density, Lightweight Alloys: Incorporate high-density alloys or metal foams for necessary radiation shielding with reduced mass.

    • Elastic Fabric Panels: Include flexible fabric sections at major joints (e.g., shoulders, hips).

  4. Weight Support
    • Shoulder Harness or Suspension System: Attach adjustable straps or a harness to redistribute weight.
    • Load-Bearing Belt: Use a supportive waist belt to transfer weight to the hips.

    • Built-In Shoulder and Waist Padding: Use ergonomically shaped padding to distribute weight evenly.
    • Cross-Body Strap Configuration: Incorporate straps for balanced load distribution across the torso.

    • Pressure-Relieving Foam Padding: Add memory foam in high-stress areas for extended comfort.
    • Adjustable Shoulder Pads: Allow customization of shoulder padding thickness.

  9. Ventilation
    • Mesh Ventilation Panels: Add mesh in heat-prone areas (e.g., back, underarms).
    • Active Cooling System: Integrate small, battery-operated cooling components.
    • Breathable Layering: Design with inner airflow-enhancing layers.

  10. Size and Fit Adjustment
    • Adjustable Straps and Closures: Include straps and closures for body-type customization.
    • Elasticized Waist and Side Panels: Use elastic sections for a snug, adaptable fit.
